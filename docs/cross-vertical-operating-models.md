# Cross-Vertical Operating Models

## Core Principle

The same operating stack applies across field and project-based service businesses:

- lead capture
- scheduling and dispatch
- CRM and customer history
- price book or estimating
- invoicing
- payments
- reporting
- follow-up automation
- memberships, warranties, or renewals

The priority order remains mostly stable. What changes by vertical is the shape of the job, the scheduling style, the documentation burden, and the repeat-service pattern.

## Now Represented In The System

The runtime now exposes first-class operating models for:

- plumbing
- mold remediation
- HVAC
- cleaning
- restoration

These are available through:

- the niche catalog
- the embed manifest
- the widget boot payload
- generated deployment packages

## Why This Matters

This makes the system more than a plumbing-specific messaging engine.

It gives LeadOS a real product model for:

- service-phase differences
- dispatch vs project scheduling differences
- flat-rate vs scope-based pricing differences
- documentation and liability needs
- repeat-revenue and follow-up differences

## Examples

### Plumbing

- call-to-cash with strong dispatch and flat-rate quoting
- urgent and same-day service pressure
- maintenance-plan and repeat-service value

### Mold remediation

- assessment -> scope -> remediation -> clearance flow
- heavier documentation and property history
- project scheduling and crew allocation instead of only same-day dispatch

### HVAC

- mixed emergency dispatch and maintenance scheduling
- equipment history is central
- maintenance agreements are a major retention layer

### Cleaning

- recurring route and slot management
- checklist-driven field completion
- retention and recurring contracts matter more than one-time dispatch urgency

### Restoration

- emergency intake plus multi-phase project execution
- project and documentation intensity
- strong need for structured follow-up and financial visibility by phase

## Product Implication

LeadOS should continue evolving toward:

- universal operating priorities
- vertical-specific operating models
- shared system capabilities
- vertical-specific execution surfaces

That keeps the platform scalable without collapsing every vertical into generic “home services” messaging.
